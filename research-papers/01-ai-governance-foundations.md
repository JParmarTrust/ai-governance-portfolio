# Executive Summary

Artificial Intelligence (AI) is rapidly transforming industries, governments, and society. Organizations are increasingly adopting AI-powered technologies to improve efficiency, automate processes, enhance decision-making, and create new business opportunities.

While AI offers significant benefits, it also introduces new risks involving privacy, security, bias, transparency, accountability, and regulatory compliance.

AI Governance provides organizations with a structured approach to managing these risks while enabling responsible innovation. This paper introduces foundational AI governance concepts and summarizes key guidance from the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) Artificial Intelligence Risk Management Framework (AI RMF), the European Union Artificial Intelligence Act (EU AI Act), and ISO/IEC 42001.

# Purpose

The purpose of this document is to provide an introduction to AI Governance, AI Risk Management, and leading governance frameworks that organizations can use to guide the responsible development, deployment, and use of Artificial Intelligence.

# What is AI Governance?

AI Governance is the system of policies, processes, controls, oversight mechanisms, and accountability structures used to ensure that Artificial Intelligence systems are developed, deployed, and operated responsibly.

Effective AI Governance helps organizations:

* Manage AI-related risks
* Protect individuals and organizations
* Support regulatory compliance
* Promote transparency and accountability
* Improve trust in AI systems
* Enable responsible innovation

AI Governance is not intended to prevent AI adoption. Instead, it seeks to ensure that AI technologies are used safely, ethically, and responsibly.

---

# Why AI Governance Matters

Unlike traditional software systems, AI systems can learn from data, adapt over time, generate unpredictable outputs, and influence real-world decisions.

Potential risks include:

* Inaccurate outputs
* Hallucinations
* Bias and discrimination
* Privacy violations
* Security vulnerabilities
* Regulatory non-compliance
* Reputational damage
* Loss of stakeholder trust

Organizations that fail to govern AI effectively may face operational, legal, financial, and reputational consequences.

# Core Principles of Trustworthy AI

The NIST AI Risk Management Framework identifies several characteristics commonly associated with trustworthy AI systems.

## Valid and Reliable

AI systems should perform consistently and function as intended within their defined operating environments.

## Safe

AI systems should minimize harm to individuals, organizations, and society.

## Secure and Resilient

AI systems should be protected against attacks, misuse, unauthorized access, and operational failures.

## Accountable and Transparent

Organizations should maintain accountability for AI outcomes and provide appropriate visibility into how AI systems are used.

## Explainable and Interpretable

AI decisions should be understandable to stakeholders when appropriate.

## Privacy Enhanced

AI systems should protect personal and sensitive information throughout the AI lifecycle.

## Fair and Bias-Aware

Organizations should identify, assess, and mitigate harmful bias within AI systems.

# Key AI Governance Domains

Effective AI Governance typically addresses several major domains.

## Governance and Oversight

Defines accountability, ownership, and decision-making authority for AI systems.

## Risk Management

Identifies, assesses, monitors, and mitigates AI-related risks.

## Security

Protects AI systems, models, data, and supporting infrastructure.

## Privacy

Ensures compliance with privacy requirements and protection of personal information.

## Compliance

Supports adherence to applicable laws, regulations, and industry requirements.

## Transparency

Provides visibility into AI capabilities, limitations, and decision-making processes.

## Human Oversight

Ensures appropriate human involvement in AI-enabled decisions and actions.

# NIST AI Risk Management Framework (AI RMF)

The NIST AI RMF provides a voluntary framework for managing AI risks and improving AI trustworthiness.

The framework is organized into four core functions.

## GOVERN

Establish organizational structures, policies, processes, roles, and responsibilities for AI governance.

Examples:

* Governance policies
* Executive oversight
* Risk management processes
* Accountability structures

## MAP

Understand the context in which AI systems operate.

Examples:

* Business objectives
* Stakeholders
* Use cases
* Potential impacts

## MEASURE

Assess AI systems and associated risks.

Examples:

* Testing
* Validation
* Security assessments
* Bias evaluations
* Performance monitoring

## MANAGE

Respond to identified risks through mitigation and ongoing monitoring.

Examples:

* Risk treatment
* Control implementation
* Incident response
* Continuous improvement

# European Union AI Act

The European Union AI Act is the first comprehensive AI regulation adopted by a major jurisdiction.

The regulation follows a risk-based approach and establishes obligations based on the potential risk posed by AI systems.

## Unacceptable Risk

AI systems that are prohibited due to unacceptable societal risk.

Examples may include certain manipulative or exploitative AI practices.

## High Risk

AI systems that may significantly impact individuals, safety, or fundamental rights.

Examples include certain employment, healthcare, education, and critical infrastructure use cases.

## Limited Risk

AI systems subject to transparency requirements.

Examples may include chatbots or AI-generated content disclosures.

## Minimal Risk

AI systems presenting low levels of risk and generally subject to minimal regulatory requirements.

# ISO/IEC 42001

ISO/IEC 42001 is the first international standard specifically focused on Artificial Intelligence Management Systems (AIMS).

The standard provides organizations with a structured management system approach for governing AI.

Core concepts include:

* Governance
* Risk Management
* Leadership
* Accountability
* Continuous Improvement
* AI Lifecycle Management

ISO 42001 follows the Plan-Do-Check-Act (PDCA) model.

## Plan

Establish governance objectives, policies, and risk management processes.

## Do

Implement governance controls and operational processes.

## Check

Monitor, evaluate, and audit AI activities.

## Act

Implement corrective actions and continuous improvement activities.

# Lessons Learned

Successful AI governance programs balance innovation with responsible risk management.

Organizations should focus on:

* Clear accountability
* Human oversight
* Risk management
* Transparency
* Security
* Privacy
* Continuous improvement

AI governance is most effective when integrated into existing business, technology, security, privacy, and compliance processes.

# Conclusion

Artificial Intelligence presents significant opportunities for organizations, but it also introduces unique risks that require thoughtful governance.

Frameworks such as the NIST AI Risk Management Framework, the EU AI Act, and ISO/IEC 42001 provide practical guidance for establishing governance programs that support trustworthy, responsible, and sustainable AI adoption.

Organizations that implement strong governance practices will be better positioned to manage risk, maintain stakeholder trust, and realize the long-term benefits of AI technologies.
